Data & charts

Homelessness by the numbers

National data from HUD's Annual Homeless Assessment Report (AHAR) and Point-in-Time counts. All charts are interactive — hover for details.

National Homeless Population, 2015–2024

HUD Point-in-Time Count — one-night snapshot, January each year. 2021 count was significantly disrupted by COVID-19.

Homelessness rose 18% from 2023 to 2024, reaching 771,480 — the highest count on record since HUD began tracking in 2007.

Sheltered vs. Unsheltered, 2015–2024

People in emergency shelters, transitional housing, and Safe Havens are "sheltered." Sleeping outside, in cars, or abandoned buildings is "unsheltered."

Sheltered capacity expanded sharply in 2024 — the unsheltered share fell from 39% in 2023 to 36% in 2024, even as the overall total hit a new record high.

Who Experiences Homelessness (2023)

HUD 2023 PIT Count — national totals by subpopulation. Numbers are not mutually exclusive (a veteran family counts in multiple categories).

Chronically homeless individuals — those with long-term or repeated homelessness and a disabling condition — represent 20% of the total but consume a disproportionate share of resources.

Race: US Population vs. Homeless Population

Compares each group's share of the general US population to their share of the homeless population. Source: HUD 2023 AHAR, US Census.

The most striking disparity: Black Americans are 13% of the US population but 37% of people experiencing homelessness — a 3× overrepresentation rooted in decades of discriminatory housing policy.

How the system works

How federal homelessness funding flows

Federal dollars travel through several layers before reaching a person in need. Here's how it works — in plain language.

→

Continuum of Care (CoC)

→
Local

→
Individual
People in Need

Step 1 — Congress appropriates

Congress funds homelessness programs annually — primarily through HUD's budget. Each year, HUD publishes a Notice of Funding Opportunity (NOFO) and awards grants competitively to Continuums of Care across the country.

Step 2 — CoC plans & applies

Each of the ~400 Continuums of Care nationwide applies to HUD as a regional planning body. The CoC decides how to distribute funding across local nonprofits ("subgrantees") based on performance data and community priorities.

Step 3 — Nonprofits operate programs

Local nonprofits and public agencies receive grants to run specific programs — emergency shelters, rapid rehousing, permanent supportive housing, street outreach teams, and more. Outcomes are tracked and reported back to HUD through a shared database called HMIS.

Step 4 — Coordinated Entry connects people

Most CoCs use a Coordinated Entry (CE) system — a single assessment process that matches people to the right program. High-needs individuals are prioritized for Permanent Supportive Housing; others may receive Rapid Re-Housing assistance or outreach services.

Program glossary

What the acronyms actually mean

The homelessness system runs on acronyms. Here's plain-language explanations of the most common programs, funding streams, and system components — written for community members, advocates, and anyone who doesn't already know the system.

CoC — Continuum of Care

The regional planning body responsible for coordinating homelessness services in a city or county. There are about 400 CoCs nationwide. The CoC applies for HUD funding, sets local priorities, and oversees the Coordinated Entry system. It's not a building — it's a network of agencies working together.

CE — Coordinated Entry

The "front door" to the homelessness system. Instead of going to multiple agencies separately, a person goes through one assessment that determines their level of need and connects them to the right program. It's designed so that people with the highest needs get priority for the most intensive resources.

PSH — Permanent Supportive Housing

Permanent, affordable housing combined with on-site or linked support services — case management, mental health care, substance use support, and medical care. Designed for people with long-term, complex needs. There's no time limit on the housing, and people are not required to be sober or compliant to stay.

RRH — Rapid Re-Housing

Short-term rental assistance (typically 3–24 months) plus housing search support and case management. The goal is to move someone quickly from homelessness to their own place, then step back as they stabilize. It works best for people who can realistically cover rent independently after a period of support.

TH — Transitional Housing

Temporary housing — up to 24 months — usually with attached services like job training, substance use treatment, or life skills programs. Evidence shows mixed results compared to PSH and RRH. Most CoCs have shifted resources toward those more cost-effective models, though transitional housing remains useful in specific circumstances.

ESG — Emergency Solutions Grant

HUD funding distributed to states (and some cities) to fund emergency shelter operations, homelessness prevention, rapid re-housing, and outreach. Unlike CoC program grants, ESG flows through state government and is formula-based — every state gets an allocation, not just those that compete for it.

HOPWA — Housing Opportunities for Persons With AIDS

A HUD program specifically for people living with HIV/AIDS who are at risk of or experiencing homelessness. It funds short-term rent assistance, supportive services, and facility-based housing. Funding is formula-based and flows to cities and states with high AIDS case counts.

CDBG — Community Development Block Grant

A flexible HUD grant given to cities and counties for community development — including affordable housing, infrastructure, and anti-poverty services. Local governments control how it's spent. Some communities use CDBG to supplement homeless services, but it is not homelessness-specific and can be directed to other uses.

HMIS — Homeless Management Information System

The database system HUD requires CoCs to use to track people experiencing homelessness — program entries, exits, outcomes, and demographics. HMIS is how the system measures what's working and generates the annual Point-in-Time count data. Privacy protections apply; data is used only for service coordination and federal reporting.

PATH — Projects for Assistance in Transition from Homelessness

A SAMHSA-funded (not HUD) program for outreach, engagement, and treatment for people experiencing homelessness who have serious mental illness. PATH workers go to encampments and street locations rather than waiting for people to come in. It's a key bridge between street homelessness and more intensive services.
