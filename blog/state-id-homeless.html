How to Get a State ID or Birth Certificate When You're Homeless

By Common Ladder · May 3, 2026 · 8 min read

Without a government-issued ID, almost every door closes. You can't apply for most housing programs. You can't get a job that pays taxes. You can't open a bank account, qualify for SNAP benefits, or access most medical care. And yet, for people experiencing homelessness, getting an ID is one of the hardest things to do — because the system requires an address you don't have, documents you can't afford, and paperwork you may have lost.

This guide is for you, or for a case manager helping someone get their documents back. It breaks down every step — including how to get a birth certificate without any ID to start with, what programs can help pay for it, and Arizona-specific resources that can make the process faster.

Why ID Matters So Much

A state-issued photo ID isn't just a card. It's the key to nearly every support system in the country:

  • Housing programs — virtually every shelter, rapid rehousing program, and permanent supportive housing application requires a government ID for intake
  • Employment — federal I-9 verification requires a photo ID; without it, formal employment isn't possible
  • Benefits — Social Security, Medicaid, SNAP, and most state assistance programs require ID to verify eligibility
  • Banking — most banks and credit unions won't open an account without a government-issued photo ID
  • Healthcare — while emergency rooms cannot turn anyone away, most clinics and managed care programs require identification

Recovering an ID is often the single most high-leverage action in a person's path toward stability. Case managers frequently report that once a client gets their ID, other things begin to move.

The Catch-22 of ID Recovery

Here's the frustrating reality: most DMVs and motor vehicle offices require a birth certificate to issue a state ID. And most state vital records offices require a photo ID — or payment — to issue a birth certificate. People who are homeless often have neither.

It's a bureaucratic loop that wasn't designed with housing instability in mind. But it can be broken — it just takes knowing the right starting points. The key is that birth certificates and IDs don't have to be obtained at the same time, and there are programs specifically designed to help people navigate this catch-22 at no cost.

Step 1: Get Your Birth Certificate First

A birth certificate is the anchor document — most other IDs can be obtained once you have it. The good news is that most state vital records offices allow you to request a birth certificate without a photo ID, by using an alternative verification process or by having a caseworker or legal aid attorney submit on your behalf.

What you typically need to request a birth certificate

Requirements vary by state, but most states accept some combination of the following:

  • Your full legal name at birth and date of birth
  • The city and state where you were born
  • Your parents' full names (including mother's maiden name)
  • A completed application form (usually downloadable free online)
  • A small fee — typically $10–$25 depending on the state

What if you can't afford the fee?

Fee waivers for birth certificates are available in most states for people experiencing homelessness. In Arizona, the Department of Health Services (ADHS) offers a reduced-fee or waived-fee birth certificate for individuals who can document financial hardship — typically through a letter from a shelter or social service agency. Ask your case manager or the shelter you're staying at; many shelters keep these forms on hand.

You can also request a birth certificate by mail. The Arizona vital records request form is available at azdhs.gov. If you were born in another state, search "[state name] vital records birth certificate request" to find the right office — most accept mail requests with a money order.

Born in another state? You need to contact the vital records office in the state where you were born — not the state you're currently in. The VitalChek network (vitalchek.com) offers online ordering for many states. Some states also accept requests through the National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS).

Step 2: Get Your Social Security Card

Many DMVs require proof of a Social Security Number (SSN) in addition to a birth certificate. If you don't have your Social Security card, you can get a free replacement from the Social Security Administration (SSA). You're entitled to three free replacement cards per year.

To get a replacement card, bring your birth certificate and fill out Form SS-5 at your local SSA office, or submit it by mail. Find your nearest office at ssa.gov/locator. SSA offices do not require a photo ID if you have a birth certificate and can answer questions to verify your identity.

Step 3: Get Your State ID — The Address Problem

Most DMVs ask for "proof of address" — a utility bill, a lease, a bank statement. None of those exist if you don't have a home. This is where many people get stuck. But there are legitimate alternatives that most states and many DMVs accept:

  • Letter from a shelter or service provider — A letter on official letterhead from the shelter, mission, or transitional housing program where you're staying can often substitute for a utility bill. Many shelters provide these routinely upon request.
  • General Delivery address — The U.S. Postal Service allows anyone to receive mail at a post office using "General Delivery." Some DMVs accept a General Delivery mailing address as proof of residency.
  • P.O. Box through a nonprofit — Some organizations maintain shared P.O. Box addresses for people without fixed addresses specifically for use on government documents.
  • Certified address programs — In Arizona, CPS Energy and some other agencies maintain certified address programs for people without stable housing. Ask your case manager if your county has one.

Arizona MVD Fee Waiver Program

Arizona's Motor Vehicle Division (MVD) offers a fee waiver for state IDs for individuals experiencing homelessness. You can get a non-operating identification license (AZID) for free if you're served by a qualifying social service agency. Your case manager or the intake staff at most shelters can help you document eligibility. Visit an MVD office or servicearizona.com to start the process.

Programs That Can Help

SOAR (SSI/SSDI Outreach, Access, and Recovery)

SOAR is a federally supported program that trains case managers to help people experiencing homelessness apply for Social Security disability benefits — and it often includes help obtaining the ID and birth certificate documents needed for that application. If you have a disabling condition, ask a case manager if they're SOAR-certified. Find SOAR providers through your local CoC or at soarworks.prainc.com.

Legal Aid and Law Library Programs

Many legal aid organizations offer free document recovery services specifically for people experiencing homelessness. In Arizona, Community Legal Services (602-258-3434) and DNA People's Legal Services both provide free legal assistance including help with vital records and ID recovery. Staff attorneys can submit requests on your behalf and navigate state vital records offices when you run into roadblocks.

Homeless Management Information System (HMIS) Navigation

If you are enrolled in a CoC-funded program, your case manager has access to HMIS — the shared database of services in your area. Ask them specifically about ID recovery resources in your CoC's coordinated entry system; many CoCs have dedicated ID recovery funding as part of their HUD grant.

Your Step-by-Step Action List

  1. Gather what you know. Write down your full legal name, date of birth, city and state of birth, and your parents' full names (including mother's maiden name before marriage). This is the core information for your birth certificate request.
  2. Contact a case manager or legal aid organization. Organizations like Community Legal Services in Arizona can submit your birth certificate request for you at no cost and navigate any complications. This is often the fastest path.
  3. Request your birth certificate. If you're doing it yourself, download the request form from the vital records office in your birth state, fill it out, attach a money order for the fee (or a fee waiver letter from a shelter), and mail it in. Allow 2–6 weeks.
  4. Request a replacement Social Security card. Fill out Form SS-5 at SSA.gov and visit your local SSA office with your birth certificate. This is free and usually processed the same day.
  5. Get a letter from your shelter or service provider. Ask the shelter staff or your case manager for a letter on agency letterhead confirming your address/care relationship. This substitutes for proof of address at the MVD.
  6. Visit the Arizona MVD (or your state's DMV) with your documents. Bring: birth certificate, SSN card or proof of SSN, and your shelter/service provider letter. Ask about the fee waiver for people experiencing homelessness before paying.
  7. Use your new ID immediately. Apply for benefits you may have been waiting on — SNAP, Medicaid, housing programs, employment. Your case manager can help prioritize next steps.

A Word on Patience — and Asking for Help

This process takes time, and it shouldn't. The barriers built into ID recovery systems are a known gap in the social safety net, and advocates have been pushing for reforms for years. Some cities and states are moving toward automatic ID recovery upon shelter enrollment; Arizona is working toward better integration between the MVD and CoC systems.

In the meantime: don't try to navigate this alone if you don't have to. Case managers, legal aid attorneys, and SOAR-certified workers exist specifically to help cut through these barriers. You have a right to these documents. The information in them has always been yours — recovering access to it is just a process, and one that others have done before you.

Frequently asked questions

Which document should I get first — ID or birth certificate?

Get your birth certificate first. It is the anchor document, and most other IDs can be obtained once you have it. Birth certificates and IDs don't have to be obtained at the same time.

Can I get a birth certificate without a photo ID?

Yes. Most state vital records offices allow you to request a birth certificate without a photo ID, by using an alternative verification process or by having a caseworker or legal aid attorney submit on your behalf.

What if I can't afford the birth certificate fee?

Fee waivers for birth certificates are available in most states for people experiencing homelessness. In Arizona, ADHS offers a reduced-fee or waived-fee birth certificate for individuals who can document financial hardship, typically through a letter from a shelter or social service agency.

How do I get a state ID with no proof of address?

Legitimate alternatives that many DMVs accept include a letter on official letterhead from your shelter or service provider, a General Delivery address through the U.S. Postal Service, a P.O. Box through a nonprofit, or a certified address program.

How do I replace a lost Social Security card?

You can get a free replacement from the Social Security Administration by bringing your birth certificate and filling out Form SS-5 at your local SSA office or by mail. You're entitled to three free replacement cards per year.
