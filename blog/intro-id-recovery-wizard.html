Which Document Do I Get First? Introducing the ID Recovery Wizard

By Common Ladder · June 11, 2026 · 4 min read

Losing your ID is one of the fastest ways to get stuck. Without a state ID you can’t get into a lot of shelters, can’t start a job, can’t open a bank account, and can’t finish most benefits applications. So you go to replace it — and learn you need a birth certificate first. You go to order the birth certificate and learn you need to prove who you are. You try to set that up and learn the document has to be mailed somewhere, and you don’t have an address. Each door points to another door.

The problem usually isn’t any single step. It’s the order. People lose months going to the wrong office first, getting turned away, and starting over. That’s the gap Common Ladder’s new ID Recovery Wizard is built to close.

What it does

The wizard asks what you already have — a state ID, a birth certificate, a Social Security card, a place to receive mail — and where you were born. From that, it builds a short, ordered checklist of exactly which document to get next, why it comes in that spot, what each step unlocks, and where to go. It’s the same logic a good case manager carries in their head, written down so you can carry it too.

A few things shape the order. If you’ve held an Arizona ID or license before, you’re probably already in the state’s MVD system — which often means you can skip the birth-certificate step entirely for a replacement. The wizard catches that and collapses your plan. If you were born out of state, your birth certificate is usually the slowest piece, so it moves to the very top: start it, then work everything else while it’s in the mail. And if you have nowhere to receive mail, “set up a mailing address” jumps near the front, because every document you order has to land somewhere.

The part most people don’t know

If you’re in the Phoenix or Tucson metro, you usually don’t have to do this alone — and you usually don’t have to pay. The Homeless ID Project assists people experiencing homelessness with the whole chain: replacement state IDs and driver’s licenses, plus the birth certificates behind them. They pay the document fees, they have Tele-MVD access on site so you often skip a separate trip to the MVD, and you leave with a temporary paper ID while the real one is mailed. They’ll even start the process if you’ve lost everything and have no documents at all.

Their main Phoenix office is on the Keys to Change campus at 1125 W Jackson St, inside the Lodestar Day Resource Center, open weekday mornings. They also run walk-in days in Sunnyslope, Mesa, Tempe, and Glendale, and a Tucson site. Because hours rotate and sites are first come, first served, the wizard lists the current schedule for each — but always call or check the site before you travel. That’s why the wizard puts the Homeless ID Project at the top of nearly every Arizona plan: it’s the single move that does the most work for you.

Veterans get an extra break. Arizona waives the full state ID or driver’s license fee for veterans experiencing homelessness, and the VA can pull your service record even if your DD-214 is long gone. The wizard surfaces that path automatically when you mark that you’ve served.

How it protects you

The wizard runs entirely in your browser. There’s no account, no login, and nothing is sent to any agency or stored on a server — you apply directly with each program yourself. If you’re fleeing domestic violence, it routes you somewhere different on purpose: replacing documents can create a paper trail an abuser could follow, so the tool points you to advocates who recover ID safely, including through Arizona’s confidential address program.

When you’re done, you can print your plan or add each step to My Ladder to track it. And if you want the full written walkthrough of replacing a lost ID in Phoenix — addresses, fees, and what to bring — our companion guide, How to Replace a Lost ID in Phoenix When You’re Experiencing Homelessness, covers it in depth.

One ID is often the first rung. Once it’s in your hand, the doors that were closed — shelter, a job, a bank account, benefits — start opening in order, the same way they closed.
